# Script: The Pauses Are Where You Do the Work
**Module:** Module 1 — Topic 4, Lesson 1
**Instructor:** Simon
**Duration:** 6-8 min
**Format:** Talking head + on-screen workflow diagram showing pause points

---

## LESSON GOAL
Students understand that the workflow has three built-in pauses, why each pause exists, and what they're expected to do at each one. Pause 1 is covered in full — the three-question filter for picking the winning hypothesis. Pauses 2 and 3 are previewed so students know what's coming in Module 2. The close: the pauses are designed. That's where the human judgment lives.

## RECORDING NOTES
- Show the workflow diagram with the three pause points marked — students need to see this visually
- Pause 1 gets the most time — this is where they are right now in the course
- Pauses 2 and 3 are previews only — "you'll hit these in Module 2, here's what to expect"
- The three-question filter for Pause 1: put each question on screen as you name it
- Tone: grounding. Students just ran the workflow and saw it pause. Now they understand why.

---

## SCRIPT

### [0:00–0:45] SETUP

**[Face camera]**

"When you ran the discovery workflow, it stopped and asked you something before continuing.

That wasn't a bug. That was a design decision.

The workflow has three pauses built into it. Each one is a moment where the machine hands control back to you — because what comes next requires judgment that isn't in any data file.

Let me show you where each pause is, and what you're supposed to do when you hit it."

---

### [0:45–1:15] THE THREE PAUSES

**[On-screen: workflow diagram — three pause points marked]**

"Here's the workflow from start to finish.

Pause one: after `/eim-product-analyst` surfaces hypothesis titles. The machine stops and asks: do these align with what you know?

Pause two: inside `/hypothesis-to-prd`, during solution exploration. The machine presents three or four ways to address the problem and asks: which direction do you want to take?

Pause three: when you're writing your PRD guardrails and out-of-scope sections. The machine doesn't pause here — but this is the moment where you encode everything you know that Claude doesn't.

Three pauses. Each one is yours."

---

### [1:15–4:00] PAUSE 1 — WHICH SIGNAL IS REAL

**[Face camera]**

"Pause one is where you are right now.

Your discovery machine surfaced hypotheses. Each one has evidence, impact, and a mechanism. Statistically, they all check out. The machine is asking you to look at them before it writes the full report.

Here's what Claude doesn't know: two of those hypotheses might address a problem the engineering team is already solving. One might address a segment leadership has deprioritised. One might touch your highest-value customers — the ones you can't experiment on carelessly.

Claude found real patterns. You know which ones are worth acting on.

To make this call, run three questions.

**[On-screen: Question 1 — Evidence strength]**

One: Is the evidence credible and specific? Is the population large enough to be meaningful? Is there a comparison group that makes the signal interpretable? If not, the hypothesis isn't ready to build from — not because the idea is wrong, but because you can't defend it.

**[On-screen: Question 2 — Impact size]**

Two: Is the dollar value real and sized conservatively? Strong hypotheses show their working. If the impact estimate requires things to go right that you can't control, that's a risk, not an opportunity.

**[On-screen: Question 3 — Mechanism clarity]**

Three: Is the root cause diagnosis specific enough to act on? Can an engineer scope it? Can you measure whether it worked? 'Improve onboarding' is not a mechanism. 'Trigger an in-app prompt on day three for accounts that skipped the taste profile' — that's a mechanism.

Apply these three questions to your hypotheses. Pick the one that passes all three. Tell the machine your choice and let it write the full report.

That's Pause 1."

---

### [4:00–5:15] PAUSES 2 AND 3 — PREVIEW

**[Face camera]**

"You'll hit Pause 2 in Module 2, inside `/hypothesis-to-prd`.

The workflow will present three or four distinct ways to address your chosen hypothesis. Different surfaces, different scope, different risk profiles. It asks you to choose a direction — or combine elements — before writing a single requirement.

This is where the product direction gets set. Not by Claude. By you. The call you make here shapes everything that gets built.

**[On-screen: Pause 3 label]**

Pause 3 happens when you write the guardrails and out-of-scope sections of your PRD.

The workflow doesn't stop here — but this is where you encode the constraints Claude can't know. Engineering freezes. Segments that are off-limits. Scope that needs to stay tight because you have one sprint. None of that is in any file. It's in your head. The guardrails section is where you put it in writing so the build stays bounded."

---

### [5:15–6:30] CLOSE

**[Face camera]**

"The pauses are where you do the work.

Not the prompts. Not the files. The pauses.

When the workflow stops, that's your moment. Don't rush through it. The machine will wait. What you decide at each pause is what turns a data analysis into a product direction — something with a chosen signal, a scoped build, and constraints that reflect how your company actually operates.

Claude surfaced the patterns. You decided which ones matter, how to address them, and what the build is and isn't allowed to touch. That decision chain is yours from start to finish.

In the next lesson, you'll prepare your Company B data — and when you run the workflow on it, you'll hit Pause 1 for real. You know what to do."

**[End]**
