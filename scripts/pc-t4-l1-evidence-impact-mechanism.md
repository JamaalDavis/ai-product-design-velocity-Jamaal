## SCRIPT

### [0:00–1:30] HOOK: WHY A LEADER WANTS TO SEE THIS

**[Face camera]**

"When a senior leader looks at a product proposal, they're asking three questions.

Is this a real problem — or just something someone thinks is a problem? How much is solving it actually worth to the business? And why do we believe this specific solution will work?

Most product pitches can't answer all three. They have a hunch, maybe some anecdotal feedback, a vague sense that it matters — and a solution that feels right. That's not enough. Leaders have seen too many 'feels right' projects ship and go nowhere.

There's a simpler mental model underneath this. Data → Meaning → Action.

Action without meaning is busywork. You ship something because someone asked for it — not because you know it will move anything. Meaning without data is opinion. You have a strong conviction, but nothing to test, nothing to measure, nothing to defend when someone asks why you built this instead of something else.

When you move through Data to Meaning to Action in sequence, you end up with something different: a product argument that can be evaluated before you build it, and tested after.

EIM is a format that answers all three questions — in a structure that makes the logic visible and auditable.

Evidence: here's what the data actually shows.
Impact: here's what solving it is worth.
Mechanism: here's what we're going to build — and here's why we believe it will work.

The mechanism is a hypothesis. It's our best diagnosis of the root cause, and our best bet on the intervention that addresses it. It's not a guarantee. But it's grounded — because the evidence is real, the impact is sized from real data, and the mechanism is specific enough that we can measure whether it worked.

That's why leaders trust this format. It's not a pitch. It's a structured argument that can be tested."

---

### [1:30–2:10] E — EVIDENCE

**[On-screen text: E — Evidence]**

"E is Evidence.

What does the data actually show? Not what you think is happening. Not what a customer told you in a meeting. What do the numbers show — specifically — about which users, doing what, at what rate?

'Users churn' is not evidence. 

'36% of accounts that skip the onboarding taste profile churn within 90 days, versus 8% of accounts that complete it' — that's evidence. Specific. Measurable. Defensible."

---

### [2:10–2:50] I — IMPACT

**[On-screen text: I — Impact]**

"I is Impact.

What is this actually worth? If we solve this, how much does that move the business — in dollars, in users retained, in revenue recovered?

Impact is what turns a data observation into a business case. It answers the question every stakeholder asks: 'Why should we build this instead of the ten other things on the backlog?'

If you can't size the impact, you can't prioritise the opportunity."

---

### [2:50–3:40] M — MECHANISM

**[On-screen text: M — Mechanism]**

"M is Mechanism.

Why is this happening? And — specifically — what intervention in the product would address it?

'Improve onboarding' is not a mechanism.

'Trigger an in-app prompt on day 3 for accounts that skipped the taste profile, showing a live preview of matched recipes' — that's a mechanism. Specific enough that an engineer could scope it, a designer could spec it, and you could measure whether it worked."

---

### [3:40–4:10] THE SUMMARY

**[Face camera]**

"Together:

Evidence tells you what's happening.
Impact tells you what it's worth.
Mechanism tells you what to build.

Three questions. In that order. Every time."

Evidence. Impact. Mechanism.
