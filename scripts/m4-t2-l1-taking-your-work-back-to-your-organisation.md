# Script: Taking Your Work Back to Your Organisation
**Module:** Module 4 — Topic 2, Lesson 1
**Instructor:** Simon
**Duration:** 4-5 min
**Format:** Talking head

---

## LESSON GOAL
Students have one question to ask before presenting their work to real stakeholders at their organisation after the course: what is my audience optimising for? The evidence doesn't change. The emphasis does. This is the post-Demo-Day frame — not Demo Day prep.

## RECORDING NOTES
- Talking head only — no tool demo, no screen share needed
- This is a closing frame, not a how-to lesson. Keep it tight.
- The single question is the whole lesson — don't over-explain it
- Tone: forward-looking. Students have built something real. Now they take it somewhere.

---

## SCRIPT

### [0:00–0:30] SETUP

**[Face camera]**

"You're about to present on Demo Day. But I want to talk for a moment about what happens after that.

Because the work you've built — the EIM hypothesis, the prototype, the business case — that doesn't stay in this course. You're taking it back to your organisation. And the room you present in there is different from Demo Day."

---

### [0:30–2:30] THE ONE QUESTION

**[Face camera]**

"Before you present to any real stakeholder, ask one question.

What is this person optimising for?

Not what do they want to hear. What problem are they trying to solve? What decision are they under pressure to make well?

Some audiences are optimising for speed and learning — they want to know what you'll learn by shipping this, and how fast. Certainty is less important than momentum.

Some are optimising for impact on a number they own — churn, revenue, activation rate. They want to know what this moves, by how much, at what cost.

Some are optimising for risk reduction — they're responsible for something that can't break, and they need to know the downside is bounded before they'll say yes to anything.

Your EIM evidence answers all three questions. The evidence doesn't change. What changes is which part you lead with.

If your audience optimises for speed: lead with what you'll learn and how quickly. If they optimise for impact: lead with the dollar value and the metric it moves. If they optimise for risk: lead with the pilot evidence and the rollback plan.

One question. One emphasis shift. Same case."

---

### [2:30–4:00] THE TRANSFER

**[Face camera]**

"Here's the practical step.

After Demo Day, before you present this to anyone at your company, write down one sentence: 'My audience is optimising for [X].'

Then look at your business case. In the last lesson you wrote your product narrative: 'We found [evidence]. It's worth [impact]. The root cause is [mechanism]. Here's the prototype that addresses it.'

That's your opening. Ask: does it lead with what my audience is optimising for? If your audience wants impact — does it lead with the dollar value? If they want risk reduction — does it lead with the pilot evidence?

If yes — you're ready. If not — reorder the sentence. The evidence, impact, and mechanism are all still there. You're just choosing which one opens the room. The rest of the case stays as-is.

The EIM framework gave you the evidence, the impact estimate, and the mechanism. Your judgment about which part of that to lead with — that's what makes the presentation land.

That skill is yours now. It works on this project. It works on the next one. It works on any product decision you're asked to defend."

**[End]**
