# Script: What Strong Product Opportunities Look Like
**Module:** Module 1 — Topic 1, Lesson 1
**Instructor:** Simon
**Duration:** 5-7 min
**Format:** Screen share (Company A EIM output) + talking head

---

## LESSON GOAL
Show students what a strong product opportunity actually looks like — before they build anything. Use the Company A EIM output as the concrete example. Walk one strong hypothesis in full: specific evidence, dollar-value impact, actionable mechanism. Show a weak one side-by-side. Name the criteria. Close with: "This is what you're building toward. Your discovery machine will produce output like this."

## RECORDING NOTES
- Have the actual Company A EIM output doc open and visible
- Walk through it slowly — students are reading along
- The strong vs weak comparison is the core moment — pause on each diagnosis
- Weak hypothesis: construct this as on-screen text (typed or text overlay). Not a real document — fabricated deliberately bad example for contrast. No need to open a second file.
- Tone: analytical, specific. Teaching them to read, not just produce.
- Success indicator: "When your machine produces output like this, you're on track. When it doesn't, here's the one thing to fix first."

---

## SCRIPT

### [0:00–0:30] SETUP

**[Face camera]**

"Before we build anything, I want to show you what we're building toward.

This is the output your discovery machine produces. And I want you to be able to look at it and know immediately — is this a strong opportunity, or isn't it? Because that judgment is what separates a product team that ships the right thing from one that ships the wrong thing faster."

---

### [0:30–3:30] WALKING A STRONG HYPOTHESIS

**[Screen share: Company A EIM output — Hypothesis 1]**

"Here's the output from Company A. Let me walk through the first hypothesis in detail.

**[Highlight Evidence section]**

Evidence: '36.8% of all new accounts — 662 users — skip the taste profile. Half of those, 329 accounts, also never create a meal plan. These double-skippers churn at 45.9% within 90 days, versus 8.5% for fully-activated accounts — a 5.4x difference.'

What makes this strong? Three things.

One: it names a specific behaviour. Not 'users aren't engaging.' 'Users skip the taste profile.' That's an action with a measurable rate.

Two: it names the population. 662 accounts, 329 double-skippers. We know exactly who this is about.

Three: the comparison group. 45.9% versus 8.5%. The gap is what makes the signal meaningful. Without a comparison, the number means nothing.

**[Highlight Impact section]**

Impact: '329 accounts at 45.9% churn rate. At average ACV of $192, that's approximately $1,912 in at-risk ARR per cohort. A 25% reduction in churn for this segment recovers $478 ARR per cohort, compounding monthly.'

What makes this strong? It shows its working. It's not 'significant opportunity.' It's: here's the population, here's the rate, here's the revenue, here's what a partial improvement is worth.

You can argue with the 25% assumption. You can't argue with the structure. The calculation is auditable. That's what you want in a business case.

**[Highlight Mechanism section]**

Mechanism: 'The skip rate is driven by perceived value, not friction intolerance — the taste profile screen presents as admin overhead rather than personalisation. An intervention combining a live recipe preview panel with a persistent in-feed completion prompt would reframe the value proposition at the moment of decision.'

What makes this strong? It names the root cause diagnosis — 'perceived value, not friction intolerance.' And it names a specific surface, a specific trigger, a specific user moment. An engineer could scope this. A designer could spec it. We can measure whether it worked."

---

### [3:30–5:30] THE WEAK COMPARISON

**[Cut to on-screen text — typed out or text overlay. This is constructed for illustration, not from a real document.]**

"Now I want to show you what a weak hypothesis looks like. I'm going to construct one — same topic, same product area — so you can see the contrast directly.

This is the kind of output you'll sometimes get when the data isn't structured well enough.

Evidence: 'Users who don't complete onboarding engage less.'

That's not evidence. That's a description. Which users? What rate? Compared to what? There's no number, no segment, no comparison group. Nothing to act on.

Impact: 'Improving onboarding could reduce churn.'

Could. Not 'will reduce churn by X% for segment Y based on Z.' Could. This is a hope, not an impact estimate.

Mechanism: 'Improve the onboarding experience.'

That's a strategy, not a mechanism. Who specifically? On what screen? What does 'improve' mean? An engineer can't scope this. A designer can't spec it. You can't measure it.

**[Face camera]**

Same topic. Completely different quality. The difference isn't the idea — it's the evidence, the specificity, and the diagnosis."

---

### [5:30–6:30] THE EVALUATION FRAMEWORK

**[Face camera]**

"When your discovery machine produces output, run it through three questions.

Evidence: Is there a specific behaviour, a specific population, and a comparison group?

Impact: Does it show its working — the count, the rate, the dollar estimate?

Mechanism: Is the root cause named, and is the intervention specific enough to scope?

If all three are there, you have a hypothesis worth building from.

If one is missing — that's not a reason to scrap it. That's a question to answer. Go back to the data, run a sharper query, tighten the diagnosis.

This is what you're building toward. Your discovery machine will produce output like this — specific, evidenced, actionable. In the next lesson, I'll show you how to structure your data so it gets there."

**[End]**
