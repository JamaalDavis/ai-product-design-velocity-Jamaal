Lesson 1.2 Script : Strategy Tree - From Vision to Initiatives

![Screenshot 2026-02-09 at 5.45.39 pm.png](attachment:da251083-eaa9-4b78-b297-b1cb3ffee3a2:Screenshot_2026-02-09_at_5.45.39_pm.png)

### [0:00-0:25] HOOK: The Invisible Designer

**[VISUAL: Zoom meeting screenshot - designer silent while others talk metrics]**

"Picture this: You're in a planning meeting. Your PM's talking conversion rates. Engineering is talking infrastructure. Marketing is presenting their targets.

And the designer? You're waiting for someone to ask about the redesign you've spent weeks on.

You finally present. Everyone nods. 'Looks great.' Then immediately — straight back to metrics."

---

### [0:25-0:50] PERSONAL STORY: Why I Built the Strategy Tree

**[Face camera]**

"I've felt that gap for 18 years — as a designer, as an advisor, across companies of every size. The pattern is always the same: execs talk strategy, middle management translates it loosely, and by the time it reaches the people doing the work, the thread is gone. And when people can't connect their work to the bigger picture, they lose motivation.

So I built a framework to fix that. It's called the Strategy Tree — it connects every level from vision down to your design task. After years of iterating on it, every time I show it, the reaction is the same: 'This finally makes sense.'

By the end of this lesson, you'll have that same clarity."

---

### [0:50-1:10] BUSINESS CONTEXT: Why Companies Need Strategy

**[VISUAL 1: Simple pyramid - Vision at top, Strategy in middle, Execution at bottom]**

"Companies need structure to turn vision into action. Vision at the top — the big inspiring why. Strategy in the middle — the directional choices. Execution at the bottom — where your design work lives.

The Strategy Tree connects all three. Let me show you how."

---

### [1:10-5:00] CORE TEACHING: The Strategy Tree Framework

**[Screen share: Strategy Tree diagram]**

I'm going to walk through each level top to bottom using Spotify as our example.

> 🎵 **Quick context on Spotify:** Music-only platform in 2008. Then 2019–2021 they spent $1B+ acquiring podcast companies, signed Joe Rogan, pushed into audiobooks. Today: 600M+ users, full audio platform. Their strategic pivot is textbook — and you'll see exactly how it forced every level of this tree to evolve.
> 

---

**LEVEL 1: Vision & Mission[Point to top of tree]**

"Starting at the top — vision and mission. These rarely change. Think 5–10 years.

Spotify's vision: **'To unlock the potential of human creativity — giving a million artists the opportunity to live off their art, and billions of fans the opportunity to enjoy it.'**

This sets the direction. Everything below flows from here."

---

**LEVEL 2: Strategic Direction & Pillars[Point to second level]**

"Next level down — strategic pillars. This is the company's 3–5 year focus. Here are Spotify's key strategic priorities drawn from their [investor communications](https://investors.spotify.com/home/default.aspx):

- **Scale marketplace** — grow both listeners and creators globally
- **Expand content formats** — podcasts, audiobooks, video podcasts
- **Personalization at scale** — use AI to improve discovery
- **Convert free to Premium** — improve subscription conversion
- **Improve profitability** — gross margin expansion

Their stated goal: **one billion users and 50 million creators.**

Quick note — in fast-moving markets, these pillars can shift in 6–12 months. The tree structure stays the same, the branches just grow faster."

---

**LEVEL 3: Business OKRs (Annual)[Point to third level]**

"This is where strategy becomes measurable — and it's almost always financial. Grow revenue. Cut costs. Capture market share.

For Spotify: they're targeting 30–35% gross margin in music, with long-term podcast margins of 40–50%. These are the numbers the C-suite is accountable for."

---

**LEVEL 4: Product North Star Metric (Annual)[Point to fourth level]**

"Now here's the key level for product teams. The North Star Metric is the **one product metric** that best predicts business success. Business OKRs tell you *what* to achieve. The North Star tells the product team *what to move* to get there.

Spotify's North Star: **Minutes listened per user.**

**[Face camera]**

Why is this a great North Star? Because it works both ways. For the **customer** — more minutes means they're finding content they love. For the **business** — more minutes means higher retention, lower churn, more Premium conversions.

One number — proxy for customer value *and* business health."

---

**LEVEL 5: Quarterly Product Goals → Design Tasks[Point to bottom level]**

"And here's where you live. Product breaks the North Star into quarterly goals. Your design tasks connect directly to those goals.

So let's trace the full chain:

Vision → 'audio entertainment for everyone'

Strategy → expand beyond music

Business OKR → grow ARR 25%

North Star → minutes listened per user

Q2 goal → increase podcast adoption

**Your design task → redesign podcast discovery flow**

Design metrics to track → *Podcast carousel click-through rate* and *Time from app open to first podcast play*

That's it. Every design task connects to a business metric. The tree makes it visible."

---

### [5:00-6:30] PRODUCT JOURNEY MAPPING: Connecting Design Metrics

**[Screen share: Product Journey + Metrics Mapping]**

"Most products follow this journey: Awareness → Acquisition → Activation → Retention → Advocacy.

At every stage there's a product metric the business tracks. The product teams at different journey point would work on: what can we do to move this metric? Some of those initiatives become design work — and that's where you define your design metrics.

**[Point to ACTIVATION]**

Let me show you an example. At Activation — this is where new users start engaging with content. The product metric here is **'time to first listen.'** The team asks: how do we move this? Maybe it's improving the discovery carousel, or redesigning podcast highlights.

Your design goal: **get users to their first podcast faster.** Your design metrics: time to first click, satisfaction ratings. When you reduce that time, you move a business number.

**[Face camera]**

Once you know which journey stage your work sits in, you know exactly what the business is measuring — and that changes how you show up."|

If you put in the work to connect the dots broadly, it will reshape how you frame your work 

---

### [6:30-8:00] Upgrade Your Strategic Framing

**[Screen share: Before/After Strategic Framing]**

**BEFORE:** 'I redesigned the onboarding flow. Here are the screens. What do you think?'

**AFTER:** 'Our North Star is minutes listened. We're in Activation — specifically, time to first listen. My hypothesis: if we surface podcast recommendations earlier in onboarding, we cut time-to-first-listen in half. Here's the design.'

**[Face camera]**

Same work. Completely different conversation. This sums up your strategic thinking process at a different level

Pro-tip: before your next design review, show the OKR > the journey stage> Product goal and your design hypothesis *before* you show any screens."

---

### [8:00-8:45] WRAP: Practical excercise

**[Face camera]**

"Let’s get practical about this: pick one project you're working on right now. Spend 15 minutes mapping it through the Strategy Tree — which OKR does it support? Which journey stage? What's your design hypothesis?

Next time you're in that strategy meeting and everyone's talking metrics? You won't be waiting to be asked. You'll already know where your work fits.

See you in the next one."
