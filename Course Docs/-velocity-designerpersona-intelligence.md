## AI Product & Design Velocity — Pre-Launch Audience Signal
**Sources:** Goal submissions, mid-course takeaways, and end-of-course reflections from Rupa's cohort (Claude Code for Designers). Jac attended as student-researcher.
**Last updated:** April 19, 2026
**Compiled by:** Jac Ding + Claude
**Privacy:** All student names replaced with pseudonyms. Quotes kept verbatim.

---

## HOW TO USE THIS DOC

This is not a marketing persona built from assumptions. It is audience intelligence built from real designers who enrolled in a comparable course, paid real money, showed up, and told us exactly what they came for, what cracked open for them, and what they're taking with them.

These students represent one key target archetype for Velocity. Use this doc to:
- Pressure-test course copy and lesson framing against real designer language
- Identify the aha moments worth engineering deliberately into Velocity sessions
- Borrow the exact words and phrases students use to describe their own goals and pain
- Understand what archetypes will show up in our cohort and what each one needs

Update this doc when new signals arrive from our own cohort once live.

---

## WHAT THIS ANALYSIS TELLS US

Four things we're pulling from this research:

1. **Designer goals** — what they're actually trying to accomplish, in their own words
2. **Designer pain points** — what they want to break through, not what we assume they want
3. **Designer aha moments** — the breakthroughs worth engineering into Velocity sessions
4. **Language and phrases** — exact words to use in course description, marketing, and lesson design

## PART 1: DESIGNER GOALS

*27 students submitted pre-course goals. These are verbatim signals, not paraphrases.*

---

### The Six Goal Archetypes

Six distinct motivational archetypes emerged. Most students straddle two.

---

**Archetype 1: The Bottleneck Avoider**
*"I don't want to be the reason the team slows down."*

Engineering is moving. Product is moving. This designer is watching the gap widen and enrolled to close it before it becomes visible.

Key verbatims:
> "I'd like to learn how to collaborate with them by using Claude Code myself... I don't want to be the Design bottleneck then." — Jenny

> "I want to use AI... so my team and I can gut-check and test product ideas faster (and hopefully build confidence with leadership)." — Cara

> "I want to get really comfortable using Claude since a lot of my dev/pm peers are already using it." — Oanh

**What this means for Velocity:** Early wins need to be team-visible, not just personal. A working prototype they can show in a standup the next morning is worth more than a week of solo skill-building.

---

**Archetype 2: The Ownership Seeker**
*"I want to ship it myself, not hand it off and hope for the best."*

Tired of the handoff lag, the two-pixel conversations, the gap between design intent and shipped product. Wants direct ownership of implementation — not just influence over it.

Key verbatims:
> "I want to take direct ownership. I want to push PRs myself with design audit changes, automate the audit process through Figma MCP, and stop relying on back-and-forth to get things right." — Karo

> "I want to take more direct ownership and be a full actor in the implementation phase... I also want to teach myself how to prototype, build and design directly in code, skipping the Figma canvas altogether." — Flora

> "I'm excited to adopt a new approach to building, prototyping, testing, and validating concepts." — Kelly R

**What this means for Velocity:** This student will be frustrated by anything that feels like a new handoff — even to Claude. They need to feel like the author, not the prompter. "You decide what to build, Claude builds it" lands better than "delegate to Claude."

---

**Archetype 3: The Team Transformer**
*"I'm learning this so I can bring it back."*

Learning is instrumental. The real goal is organisational change — moving the team, influencing the practice, becoming the person who knows what to do with AI.

Key verbatims:
> "On top of my personal goals, some of what I am tasked with at work is to help guide my fellow designers through this AI transformation. This makes it crucial that I know what I'm talking about." — Bri

> "I want to create workflows that I can reuse and share with my team, so we can work more efficiently." — Oanh

> "I want to develop a stronger perspective on how best to leverage Claude Code... a group of designers will be transitioning to report to me." — Lauren

> "My team just got access to Claude Code so I'm hoping this will help to accelerate our learning." — Pam

> "I want to develop the skills I need to stay relevant... I'm also keen to share my knowledge with other designers in my company." — Jules

**What this means for Velocity:** This student needs frameworks, not just workflows. They need to be able to explain what they learned, not just demonstrate it. Skill files are particularly valuable to them — they're inherently shareable. At least 5 of 27 students in this cohort enrolled to bring this back to their org. Bigger archetype than expected.

---

**Archetype 4: The Personal Builder**
*"I have an idea I want to ship."*

Not just improving their day job. Has a product, a SaaS idea, a portfolio project, or a tool they want to build for themselves. The course is the catalyst to make it real.

Key verbatims:
> "I have a couple application ideas I am interested in building so hoping to learn some foundational pieces that will allow me to create prototypes." — Marcus

> "My big goal is to ship my SaaS product by the end of June 2026." — Anna

> "I might want to build my own product in the future." — Jules

> "I'd love to learn how to ship apps live without needing to code!" — Cait

**What this means for Velocity:** The Company B structure (students' own data) directly serves this archetype. They bring the most intrinsic motivation. They're also the most likely to hit "this doesn't apply to me" if demos feel too enterprise. Keep Fieldwork grounded and the Company B handoff explicit.

---

**Archetype 5: The System Builder**
*"I want a repeatable workflow, not a one-time trick."*

Less interested in any single output. Wants the process, the template, the system they can run again next week without starting over. Often arrives with the most sophisticated pre-existing mental model.

Key verbatims:
> "Dedicate time to thinking about workflows and create sample projects to support my team as we leverage AI in smart ways." — Kelley

> "I want to understand deeply each point of the PDLC and learn the reusable workflow." — Maha

> "My biggest aha moment was that I could use a combination of MD files and skills to lay a foundation... and operationalize my process." — Shannon

> "The goal is to move faster from a blank page to a strong direction... build a workflow I can use myself and bring to my team." — Dave

**What this means for Velocity:** Skill files, CLAUDE.md, and the reusable discovery machine are this student's primary deliverable — not the prototype itself. They need to understand the system logic well enough to modify it, not just run it.

---

**Archetype 6: The Relevance Seeker**
*"I can see the industry shifting. I need to shift with it."*

Motivated by career survival and identity preservation rather than a specific project or team goal. Honest about the anxiety. Shows up because not showing up feels riskier.

Key verbatims:
> "I aim to obtain the knowledge that is necessary to stay relevant in my role at a company that mandates AI to be used for everything." — Alex

> "I want to better understand what this means for my career... I think I am still processing so many things." — Judy

> "I joined this course to learn how to use AI in a structured, intentional way in my design workflow — not as a gimmick but as real support for better thinking." — Maria

**What this means for Velocity:** This student needs identity reassurance alongside skill-building. They need to know they're not just learning a tool — they're becoming a different kind of professional. The "strategist, not engineer" framing matters most to them.

## PART 2: DESIGNER PAIN POINTS

### The Real Fears (in their exact words)

Use these in course copy, welcome messaging, and Session 1 framing.

**Fear of being the bottleneck:**
- "I don't want to be the Design bottleneck"
- "I don't want to be the reason they slow down"
- Engineering is already moving without them

**Fear of the tool itself:**
- "Claude Code is a bit intimidating to me and I don't want that to be the case"
- "That is daunting" (said at course end, still — about a personal project)
- The technical environment is a source of anxiety before a line is written

**Fear of irrelevance:**
- "Stay relevant in my role at a company that mandates AI"
- "Stay up to date with the latest design tools in the industry"
- "Better understand what this means for my career"

**Fear of surface-level adoption:**
- "Not as a gimmick but as real support for better thinking"
- "I feel exceptionally competent in vibe coding... but Claude Code is a bit intimidating"
- They've seen AI produce generic output and don't want to produce more of it

**Fear of not being able to repeat it:**
- "I want reusable workflows" (repeated across many students)
- The anxiety isn't just "can I do this" — it's "can I do this again next time without help"

---

### The Structural Pain Points

Beyond individual fears, several structural problems appeared across multiple students.

**The handoff problem:**
> "Audits feel like handholding: chasing down devs over two-pixel misalignments and nudging implementations that were never quite pixel-perfect to begin with." — Karo

This is the designer-developer relationship broken at the implementation layer. Students don't just want to work faster — they want to eliminate the communication overhead entirely.

**The intake problem:**
> "One institutional problem has persisted, bafflingly immune to attempts at fixing... Lack of structured intake. That leads to reverse-engineering product intent, inferring user needs from biased, internal sources." — Andreas

A more sophisticated pain point: the problem isn't building — it's that nobody defined what to build before the building started. This is exactly the EIM gap.

**The generic output problem:**
> "I want to move faster from a blank page to a strong direction, explore more angles than I normally would, and still keep everything aligned with our brand and standards." — Dave

Generic AI output that doesn't match brand standards is the silent failure mode. Students sense this as a problem before they can name it.

## PART 3: DESIGNER AHA MOMENTS

*Mid-course takeaways and end-of-course reflections from Rupa's cohort. These are the breakthroughs worth engineering deliberately into Velocity sessions.*

---

### Aha 1: Structure over prompts

The dominant breakthrough. Arrived independently across multiple students. The shift from "prompt and hope" to "build the system, get consistent results."

> "My biggest aha moment was that I could use a combination of MD files and skills to lay a foundation to get better results, work more efficiently, and operationalize my process." — Shannon

> "The 'aha' moment was seeing how keeping Claude.md updated, embedded and consistently referenced in the workspace creates continuity... not as a one-off prompt interface, but as something you have to actively scaffold to get consistent results." — Flora

> "My main takeaway so far is that good AI output depends a lot on the setup behind it." — Maria

**Use in Velocity:** Name this as a principle in Session 1, not after students discover it by accident. Candidate framing: "The setup is the skill."

---

### Aha 2: Skills collapse the complexity

The design system generator skill produced a specific before/after moment worth replicating.

> "My take away was 'This is sooooooo much work! How is this more efficient!?' [...] My aha moment was using the design system generator skill. When I used a skill, the solution took only moments! [...] I am shocked at how fast this all was and the quality of output!" — Bri

The insight: the process sounds overwhelming when described. Doing it with the right skill takes minutes. Students need to do it before they believe it.

**Use in Velocity:** Pre-empt this in pre-course materials. Name the gap explicitly: "Everything in this course sounds complicated until you do it. The first time you run a skill, you'll wonder why you were scared."

---

### Aha 3: The autonomous shift

Multiple students described a moment where they stopped following the lesson and started directing Claude themselves. Nobody had language for it. It emerged from friction and repetition — not by design.

> "My biggest aha moment was not just seeing something work based on the instructions given. It was the moment my mind started to think about manipulating what was given and rethinking how I can actually apply this to my own work every day." — Dave

> "I didn't even have to build the perfect skill — I needed to simply find and leverage the ideal skill." — Bri

**Use in Velocity:** Engineer this as a named moment in Session 2 rather than leaving it to chance. Candidate language: "This is where you stop following prompts and start directing. You'll know when it happens. It feels like deciding instead of guessing."

---

### Aha 4: Context architecture as superpower

One student at course end independently named "context architecture" and experienced it as a capability she didn't know she needed — the clearest signal that it landed as a real mental model, not just a lesson title.

> "What excites me most is experimenting — especially building design systems that Claude can actually use as a foundation. The idea that you can shape the visual direction and feed Claude the right context so it builds with your aesthetic, not against it — that's the part I can't wait to play with. Context architecture felt like unlocking a superpower I didn't know I needed." — Jules

**Use in Velocity:** This is a course marketing asset in student language. Make "context architecture" a named, taught concept with its own lesson rather than letting it emerge as a side effect of the design system module.

---

### Aha 5: Component spec as the missing layer

A more advanced aha — moving from component libraries to specs as the right input for AI-assisted design.

> "The 3-step framework (design system, tokens, component spec)... The component spec, which goes beyond design tokens and includes intent and use cases for each component, is a game-changer. This enables transitioning from manual component creation to automated design, where designs can be built by describing the intent and purpose of a screen or flow." — Anna

**Use in Velocity:** Maps directly to the DS fidelity check lesson (Post-S2). Surface the "spec over file" framing explicitly in Session 2 rather than assuming students will arrive there on their own.

---

### Aha 6: The environment shift (Terminal vs VS Code)

Not a conceptual aha — a relief moment. Multiple students flagged the move from Terminal to VS Code as a specific turning point.

> "Moving from working in Terminal to VS Code... managing projects and my file structure is WAY easier. Your layer cake analogy is much easier to apply when you are opening files in VS Code than I found it be to be doing commands in Terminal." — Marcus

**Use in Velocity:** Velocity already uses Claude Desktop App, not Terminal. This validates that design decision. Use it as a positioning point: "We made the decision Rupa's students wish someone had made for them from the start."

### How they describe Claude (not as a tool)

Across 27 goal submissions, nobody described Claude as a "tool." They described it as:
- "creative partner"
- "design partner"
- "collaborate with"
- "leverage"

This validates the "I'm the strategist, Claude is my builder" framing. Do not use "tool" when describing Claude in student-facing content.

## PART 5: WHAT THIS MEANS FOR VELOCITY COURSE DESIGN

### Curriculum implications

**1. Name "the setup is the skill" in Session 1.**
The structure-over-prompts aha arrived for multiple students independently. Don't leave it to emerge. Name the principle before they feel it so they know what to look for.

**2. Engineer the autonomous shift in Session 2.**
Multiple students hit it through friction, not design. A named moment in Session 2 would produce this for everyone, not just students who persist long enough to discover it by accident.

**3. Pre-empt the "this sounds overwhelming" problem.**
Bri's arc (terrified by description, shocked by execution) should be named in pre-course materials. The gap between how the process sounds and how it feels is the primary conversion barrier.

**4. Surface the "spec over file" framing explicitly in Session 2.**
Anna arrived there independently. It maps to DS fidelity. Too important to leave to chance.

**5. Make "context architecture" a named concept.**
Jules named it at course end and experienced it as a superpower. It should have its own lesson or at minimum a named moment, not just emerge as a side effect.
